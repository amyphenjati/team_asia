What is DEPRESSION?

Depression is a medical disorder that causes a person to experience extreme feelings of sadness and hopelessness. It is natural to temporarily feel down from time to time, especially if you are going through a loss or stressful situation, but depression is more than that. If these feelings are intense and persist over weeks or months, and if they stop you enjoying or even doing your normal activities, it’s likely that you have depression. Some people may struggle with depression for months to years. Depression can be a serious mental illness resulting in thoughts of suicide and self-harm.

Causes of Depression

Depression is a complex illness. An imbalance in brain chemicals called neurotransmitters can be a factor of depression. Factors such as genetic characteristics allow one to be vulnerable to developing depression than others.

Types of Depression

There are several types of depression. The most common include:
  • Major Depression — severe symptoms that interfere with your ability to work, sleep, concentrate, eat, and enjoy life. A person may experience multiple episodes.
  • Persistent Depressive Disorder (Dysthymia) — depression symptoms that are less severe than those of major depression. Dysthymia last longer than major despression, usually at least two years.
  • Minor Depression - depression symptoms that are less severe than those of major depression and dysthymia, and symptoms do not last long.

How to Get Help

You may feel reluctant or embarrassed to seek advice, but remember that depression is common, and getting help for depression is not a sign of weakness.
Depression is not something you can treat on your own, and it is important that you seek help from mental health professional. The right treatment may help improve your overall health and quality of life. With the right treatment, you may begin to see improvements as early as two weeks from the start of your therapy. Some symptoms may start to improve within a week or two, but it may be several weeks before you feel the full effect.

If you are thinking about harming yourself, you should take action immediately.

Emergency Action

  • Call you doctor
  • Call 911 for emergency service
  • Go to the nearest hospital emergency room
  • Call National Suicide Prevention Lifeline(800-273-8255) to connected to a trained counselor at a suicide crisis center nearest you
